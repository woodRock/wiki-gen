TurboQuant is a vector quantization algorithm introduced by Zandieh et al. (2025) from Google Research, NYU, and Google DeepMind, designed to achieve near-optimal distortion rates for both mean-squared error (MSE) and inner product preservation. The method randomly rotates input vectors to induce a concentrated codebook construction.">beta distribution on coordinates, then applies analytically optimal scalar quantizers per coordinate, achieving within a small constant factor (≈2.7×) of the information-theoretic lower bound on distortion at any bit-width. For kv cache quantization, TurboQuant achieves quality neutrality at 3.5 bits per channel and marginal degradation at 2.5 bits, while reducing nearest-neighbor search indexing time to virtually zero.

Vector Quantization and the Distortion-Rate Problem

vector quantization (vq) is the problem of compressing high-dimensional floating-point vectors to low-bitwidth integers while minimizing distortion in their geometric structure. Rooted in Shannon's source coding theory, VQ is fundamental to modern AI inference, large language model KV caching, and nearest-neighbor search in vector databases.

The information-theoretic limit on achievable distortion is defined by the Shannon distortion-rate function, which specifies the minimum MSE achievable by any quantizer at a given bit rate. Achieving or approaching this bound in practice requires both theoretical insight and algorithmically efficient implementation.

Existing vector quantization methods fall into two broad categories. Data-dependent methods like codebooks per sub-vector via k-means. Requires expensive offline training; TurboQuant replaces this with analytical codebooks.">product quantization (pq) learn a codebook from training data and achieve excellent distortion but require expensive offline training (k-means over the dataset) and are unsuitable for online applications like kv cache quantization where vectors arrive sequentially at inference time.

Data-oblivious methods are suitable for online use but typically achieve suboptimal distortion, sometimes by exponential factors in their dependence on bit-width. TurboQuant bridges this gap by providing a data-oblivious algorithm with near-optimal distortion rates provably within a small constant factor of the information-theoretic bound.

Random Rotation and Beta-Distributed Coordinates

TurboQuant's core mechanism begins by applying a random orthogonal rotation to the input vector x ∈ ℝᵈ, producing a rotated vector x̃ = Rx where R is a random orthogonal matrix (implemented efficiently as a random Hadamard transform). This rotation uniformly distributes the energy of x across all d dimensions.

The critical insight is that each coordinate x̃ᵢ of the rotated vector follows a specific codebook construction.">beta distribution (up to scaling) when the input vector has unit norm. Specifically, x̃ᵢ² / ‖x‖² ~ Beta(1/2, (d-1)/2) for a uniformly random direction, which means each squared coordinate is Beta-distributed with known parameters depending only on dimension d.

This codebook construction.">beta distribution has two important properties for quantization. First, it has bounded support: coordinates lie in [-‖x‖, ‖x‖] with known bounds, eliminating the need to estimate scale from data. Second, neighboring coordinates are nearly independent in high dimensions due to the near-independence property of random projections, allowing coordinate-wise (scalar) quantizers to approach the performance of full vector quantizers.

The near-independence of coordinates is the key property enabling TurboQuant to apply optimal scalar quantization independently to each coordinate while achieving performance close to a full d-dimensional vector quantizer. This is a non-trivial claim: for correlated coordinates, scalar quantization is significantly suboptimal, but TurboQuant's rotation induces near-independence.

Optimal Lloyd-Max Scalar Quantization per Coordinate

Given the known codebook construction.">beta distribution of each coordinate after rotation, TurboQuant applies the Lloyd-Max optimal scalar quantizer for this specific distribution. The lloyd-max quantizer minimizes MSE for a scalar source with known distribution by solving a continuous 1D k-means problem: it partitions the scalar range into 2^b intervals and assigns each interval a representative value such that the expected squared reconstruction error is minimized.

For the Beta(1/2, (d-1)/2) distribution, the Lloyd-Max optimal quantizer boundaries and reconstruction values can be precomputed numerically and stored as a lookup table. This table is the same for all vectors regardless of their specific values (depending only on d and b), making the quantization both data-oblivious and optimal for the known source distribution.

The MSE achieved by TurboQuant with b bits per coordinate satisfies MSE(TurboQuant) ≤ C · 2^{-2b} · ‖x‖² for a universal constant C ≈ 2.7. The information-theoretic lower bound for any vector quantizer is MSE ≥ (1/12) · 2^{-2b} · ‖x‖² in the worst case. TurboQuant differs from this bound by only the constant C, demonstrating near-optimality.

This exponential improvement over prior data-oblivious methods is significant: naive uniform quantization achieves MSE scaling as O(d · 2^{-2b}) (linear in d), and many randomized approaches achieve O(2^{-b}) (only linear in compression factor). TurboQuant achieves O(2^{-2b}) matching the fundamental limit's exponential dependence on bit-width.

Two-Stage Inner Product Quantization via QJL Residual

MSE-optimal quantization does not automatically imply unbiased inner product estimation. A key theoretical contribution of TurboQuant is proving that MSE-optimal quantizers introduce systematic bias in inner product estimation: the estimator ⟨Q(x), Q(y)⟩ is a biased estimator of ⟨x, y⟩ for MSE-optimal Q, even when the MSE distortion is small.

To obtain unbiased inner product quantization, TurboQuant uses a two-stage approach. Stage 1 applies the MSE-optimal quantizer described above, producing quantized representation Q₁(x) and residual r₁ = x - Q₁(x). Stage 2 applies the QJL transform (1-bit quantized Johnson-Lindenstrauss) to the residual r₁, producing a 1-bit sketch QJL(r₁) that can be combined with a query to yield an unbiased inner product estimator.

The combined estimator ⟨Q₁(x) + QJL(r₁), y⟩ is provably unbiased for ⟨x, y⟩ with low variance. Stage 1 captures the dominant MSE component, minimizing the residual that Stage 2 must handle. Stage 2 handles the remaining distortion with minimal bit overhead. The combination achieves both MSE optimality and inner product unbiasedness simultaneously.

This two-stage design elegantly decomposes the quantization problem: MSE and inner product distortion have different optimality conditions, and TurboQuant addresses each in sequence. The total bit cost is b bits per coordinate from Stage 1 plus 1 additional bit per projected dimension from Stage 2, a small overhead for the unbiasedness guarantee.

Applications to KV Cache and Nearest-Neighbor Search

For kv cache quantization in LLMs, TurboQuant's online nature is critical: key and value embeddings arrive sequentially during token generation and must be quantized immediately without access to future tokens. TurboQuant's data-oblivious design requires no calibration and applies directly to each token as it is generated.

Experimental results show quality-neutral kv cache quantization (no statistically significant perplexity increase) at 3.5 bits per channel across multiple LLM families. At 2.5 bits per channel, only marginal quality degradation is observed. This performance approaches state-of-the-art data-dependent methods while requiring no offline training.

For nearest-neighbor (NN) search in vector databases, TurboQuant outperforms existing codebooks per sub-vector via k-means. Requires expensive offline training; TurboQuant replaces this with analytical codebooks.">product quantization (pq) techniques in recall performance while reducing indexing time to virtually zero. Standard PQ requires running k-means over the database vectors to learn codebooks, taking hours or days for large datasets. TurboQuant's analytical codebook eliminates this entirely, enabling real-time database construction.

The near-independence property after random rotation also enables embarrassingly parallel quantization: each coordinate can be quantized independently on separate hardware threads with no communication, making TurboQuant particularly well-suited to GPU acceleration. The regular memory access patterns and fixed-width arithmetic operations further improve GPU utilization compared to codebook-based methods.

Concept Breakdown

Random Orthogonal Rotation

Multiplication of input vectors by a random orthogonal matrix (implemented as a random Hadamard transform in O(d log d)) before quantization. This rotation uniformly spreads energy across all coordinates and induces a codebook construction.">beta distribution on each coordinate, enabling optimal scalar quantization without data-dependent calibration.

Beta-Distributed Coordinate Representation

After random rotation, each squared coordinate of a unit-norm vector follows a Beta(1/2, (d-1)/2) distribution—a known, analytically characterized distribution depending only on vector dimension d. This known distribution is the foundation for constructing the optimal quantization codebook analytically.

Lloyd-Max Scalar Quantizer

The optimal scalar quantizer for a known 1D source distribution, minimizing MSE by solving a continuous k-means problem. TurboQuant applies the lloyd-max quantizer analytically derived for the Beta(1/2, (d-1)/2) distribution, precomputed as a lookup table shared across all vectors and layers.

Near-Independence of Rotated Coordinates

In high dimensions, distinct coordinates of a randomly rotated vector are nearly statistically independent, even though they all depend on the same original vector. This near-independence allows applying optimal scalar quantizers to each coordinate independently without significant loss from treating them as correlated.

QJL Residual Stage

The second stage of TurboQuant's inner product quantizer that applies the QJL (1-bit Quantized Johnson-Lindenstrauss) transform to the residual after MSE-optimal quantization. This stage corrects the systematic inner product bias introduced by MSE-optimal quantizers, yielding an unbiased combined estimator.

Information-Theoretic Lower Bound

TurboQuant provides formal proofs of lower bounds on the best achievable distortion rate for any vector quantizer, showing the fundamental limit scales as 2^{-2b} per coordinate at b bits. TurboQuant achieves within 2.7× of this bound, demonstrating near-optimality that no data-oblivious method had previously approached.
